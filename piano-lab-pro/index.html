Piano Lab Pro

Mixed reality performance preparation on Apple Vision Pro. Built at the University of Michigan's Center for Academic Innovation in partnership with the School of Music, Theatre & Dance.

Role

Product Manager

Institution

Timeline

The performance anxiety wall.

Music performance anxiety (MPA) is well-documented and widespread, and it's structurally built into how musicians train. Students rehearse alone in small practice rooms, then perform in front of live audiences in large concert halls. The gap between those two environments — the acoustics, the spatial scale, the audience presence, the social stakes — isn't something repetition in a practice room closes.

Piano students are particularly exposed. Their instrument is fixed: you can't carry your home environment with you. What you can do is make the practice environment more like the performance environment before the performance happens. That's the thesis behind Piano Lab Pro.

The finding that shaped everything

The anxiety trigger students identified most consistently wasn't the performance itself. It was the moment when the audience lights dim, the stage lights come up, and the room goes quiet — the "all eyes on me" moment. That finding became the product's core design requirement.

Hill Auditorium from the stage. This is what a student faces the moment the lights shift.

The collaboration

Piano Lab Pro is a partnership between CAI's XR team and two SMTD faculty members with complementary expertise. On the CAI side: a cross-functional team of XR developers, UX designers, learning experience designers, behavioral scientists, student fellows, and me as PM — fifteen contributors across two years, from proposal through prototype.

Discovery: what I needed to understand before we could scope anything

I coordinated several months of research before any development began. I needed to understand the problem well enough to scope it — and to push back when the scope started to drift. The project followed a structured Discovery → Framing → Inception → MVP path.

The CAI project framework: diverge to find the right problems, converge on the right solutions, then iterate toward MVP. Key activities I drove across the discovery and framing phases:

groups

Student interviews

Structured interviews across experience levels, focused specifically on what happens in the hours and minutes before a high-stakes performance: the anxiety triggers, the coping strategies tried, and what made it worse.

school

Faculty consultations

With Aya and Anıl to understand pedagogical goals, course structure, and what success would actually look like for their students — not just what they wanted to build.

Literature review

On MPA and flow state — brought into early scoping conversations to ground design decisions in evidence rather than intuition.

Site visits

To Britton Recital Hall, Hill Auditorium, and SMTD classrooms — I organized access to the actual spaces so the team understood the physical environment before designing for it.

Design jams

Students marked where they feel most anxious on a two-axis map (venue size × audience familiarity). The clustering — large venue, strangers — directly shaped the product's default scenario.

Mixed reality mastery.

The student wears an Apple Vision Pro headset while sitting at a real piano. The headset places them inside a 3D-modeled U-M concert venue. They can see their real hands and real keys through passthrough; everything else — the hall, the seats, the audience — is virtual.

Pre-performance (Default Play)

The student enters the venue with the house lights up and the audience audibly chatting. They can get acquainted with the space, adjust their setup, and mentally prepare at their own pace.

surround_sound

Spatial acoustics

Venue acoustics — reverb, room size, early reflections — applied using convolution algorithms calibrated to each real space. Piano audio routes via Bluetooth MIDI.

Performance Mode

When the student is ready, they trigger the transition themselves. The audience lights dim, the crowd quiets, stage lights isolate the student — that exact "all eyes on me" moment. Distractions are randomized across conditions.

Summary screen

At the end of a performance, a Summary screen appears in passthrough so the student can see the space without removing the headset. Shows time in performance and the number of audio and visual distractions encountered, with positive reinforcement messaging grounded in the MPA research.

Capturing Britton Recital Hall and Hill Auditorium at the fidelity the experience required was an operational and coordination problem as much as a technical one — and coordinating it was my job.

Product decisions worth calling out

handshake

ISR partnership: identifying the capability gap early

Self-reported anxiety scores weren't going to be enough to validate whether the app actually worked. Our team didn't have objective impact measurement expertise in-house, and I knew that gap would become a credibility problem at scale. I sought out a partnership with U-M's Institute for Social Research early in the project to bring biometric validation into the study design. That partnership shaped how we'd eventually measure success, and it came from reading what the team couldn't do alone — not from a feature spec. ISR received IRB approval, conducted pilot testing, and the collaboration has since opened a potential connection with the Berlin Philharmonic.

filter_1

Scoping three faculty concepts to one value proposition

When the project began, three competing faculty concepts were on the table. Each was defensible on its own. Getting to one required a lot of saying no to good ideas — narrowing not because ideas were bad but because a single differentiated thesis is more buildable, more evaluable, and more honest with stakeholders than a product trying to be everything. The hardest part was holding that line through faculty enthusiasm for their own concepts.

merge

Merging Default Play and Performance Mode into one flow

An early version offered Default Play and Performance Mode as separate menu options. Usability testing surfaced that this was wrong: the separation made the "all eyes on me" moment optional and opt-in, which undermined the whole point. The redesign merged them into a single continuous flow. The anxiety-inducing transition is now built in, not choosable.

Piano alignment: rejecting the first solution, A/B testing the second

Getting the virtual piano to align with the real piano keys — so the student's hands appear to play the real instrument — was harder than it looked. The first solution (manipulate a virtual cutout to match the real keys) violated standard XR interaction patterns and consistently failed in usability testing. Rather than push the team to iterate on a broken pattern, I made the call to prototype a second approach: a transparent "cuboid" overlaid on the real keys. We ran an A/B test on both. Results were mixed enough that neither won outright, and the team is now designing a hybrid. The lesson: sometimes the first workable solution isn't the right one to ship.

Where it stands

As of April 2026, the project is building toward an MVP target of June 2026. The working prototype runs on the Apple Vision Pro and includes Britton Recital Hall and Hill Auditorium environments, a live audience, Bluetooth MIDI audio routing, the pre-performance to performance flow, and a summary screen. ISR's IRB approval is in place and pilot testing has been conducted.

Outstanding work includes completing the alignment UX, the onboarding sequence, and the summary screen content. The student-facing preparation guide has been built and deployed as a standalone Google Site. The instructor-side tooling — the half of the product that's upstream of the student experience — is planned but not yet built.

Reflections

What I learned

Holding the line on scope

Scoping three competing faculty concepts down to one differentiated value proposition is a PM decision that looks like a writing exercise until you've tried it with real faculty who have real enthusiasm for their own ideas. Holding that line matters.

Reading what the team can't do

A PM who shows up to a biometric research conversation having already identified the gap and already found the right people is doing something different than one who waits to be told what the product needs. The ISR partnership happened because I read what the team couldn't do, not because someone handed me a research spec.
