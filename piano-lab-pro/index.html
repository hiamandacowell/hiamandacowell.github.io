The problem.

Music performance anxiety is well-documented, widespread, and structurally built into how musicians train. Students rehearse alone in small practice rooms, then perform in large concert halls in front of live audiences. Repetition in a practice room doesn't close that gap.

Piano students are particularly exposed. Their instrument is fixed: you can't carry your home environment with you. What you can do is make the practice environment more like the performance environment before the performance happens.

Key finding from interviews

The strongest signal wasn't about the performance itself. It was the moment the lights shift, the audience quiets, and the room changes. That moment became the product's core design requirement.

Hill Auditorium from the stage. What a student faces the moment the lights shift.

From three ideas to one product.

Two years, a small core team, and 35+ contributors overall. The XR developers, UX designers, learning experience designers, behavioral scientists, and student fellows came from CAI; the two faculty partners (Aya Hagelthorn and Anıl Çamcı, both at the School of Music, Theatre & Dance) came with three competing concepts that each could have been their own product. I led the discovery work, scoped from three ideas to one, and stayed with the build through prototype.

CAI process and the dot-voted learner goals that anchored every decision.

The student wears Apple Vision Pro at a real piano. Through passthrough they see their own hands on real keys. Everything else is virtual: Hill Auditorium or Britton Recital Hall, a live audience, calibrated venue reverb. When they're ready, the student triggers the transition: lights shift, the crowd quiets, the stage is lit. That "all eyes on me" moment is now part of the practice session.

The student wears the headset at a real piano. The lights shift, the audience quiets, and the practice session becomes the moment they fear most.

Building it (and what testing changed).

Three major moments shaped by users: which venues, simplifying the flow, and how to handle piano alignment.

Students mapped where they feel most anxious by venue size and audience familiarity. Two clusters emerged that drove the decision to build both Hill Auditorium and Britton Recital Hall.

Students were asked to mark where they felt most anxious on a two-axis grid: venue size and audience familiarity. The dots clustered in two corners. Large venue, strangers. Small venue, familiar audience.

Those two clusters drove the decision to build both Hill Auditorium and Britton Recital Hall, rather than picking one and assuming it generalized.

Additionally, Default Play and Performance Mode, originally separate menu options, were merged into a single continuous flow once testing showed a strong desire to orient themselves in the venue before starting a performance.

Similarly, the original piano alignment approach was clunky in usability sessions and didn't match XR alignment best practices; the team built an alternative and A/B tested the two against each other. Ultimately the evidence pointed toward a third hybrid version of the two.

Building both venues at the fidelity the experience required took real production work.
