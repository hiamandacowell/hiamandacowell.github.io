The problem.

Music performance anxiety is well-documented, widespread, and structurally built into how musicians train. Students rehearse alone in small practice rooms, then perform in large concert halls in front of live audiences. Repetition in a practice room doesn't close that gap.

Piano students are particularly exposed. Their instrument is fixed: you can't carry your home environment with you. What you can do is make the practice environment more like the performance environment before the performance happens.

Key finding from interviews

The strongest signal wasn't about the performance itself. It was the moment the lights shift, the audience quiets, and the room changes. That moment became the product's core design requirement.

Hill Auditorium from the stage. This is what a student faces the moment the lights shift.

From three ideas to one product.

Two years, a small core team, and 35+ contributors overall. The XR developers, UX designers, learning experience designers, behavioral scientists, and student fellows came from CAI; the two faculty partners (Aya Hagelthorn at the School of Music, Theatre & Dance, and Anıl Çamcı in Performing Arts Technology) came with three competing concepts that each could have been their own product. I was the product manager across all of it, proposal through prototype.

The project followed a structured Discovery → Framing → Inception → MVP path. I led several months of research before development began so the team could scope with confidence and decide from evidence. Two artifacts mattered most: the CAI process diagram below, and the dot-voted set of learner goals from the design jam.

The top three goals — feeling confident through distracting audiences, building pre-performance routines, and exploring unfamiliar performance environments — became the only goals features had to map to from that point forward.

Diverge to find the right problems, converge on the right solutions, then iterate toward MVP.

Design jam dot vote: the learner goals that anchored every product decision.

The student wears Apple Vision Pro at a real piano. Through passthrough they see their own hands on real keys. Everything else is virtual: Hill Auditorium or Britton Recital Hall, a live audience, calibrated venue reverb.

When they're ready, the student triggers the transition: lights shift, the crowd quiets, the stage is lit. That "all eyes on me" moment is now part of the practice session.

Building it (and what testing changed).

Capturing two real auditoriums at the fidelity the experience required was an operational problem. Running it was my job. The numbers below are what a faithful virtual concert hall actually costs in logistics.

What testing changed

Two product decisions came directly from testing findings.

Default Play and Performance Mode, originally separate menu options, were merged into a single continuous flow. Testing showed the separation made the anxiety-triggering transition optional, which defeated the point of the product.

The original piano alignment approach failed consistently in usability sessions. Rather than iterate on a broken pattern, I made the call to prototype an alternative and A/B test them against each other.

Validation infrastructure, forward-looking

Self-reported anxiety scores wouldn't be enough to claim the app actually moves the needle. Early in the project I sought out a partnership with U-M's Institute for Social Research to design biometric validation into the eventual study. IRB approval is in place, pilot testing has been conducted, and the full impact study runs post-launch. The scaffolding is there; the evidence comes once students use it in the wild.

Decisions and their costs.

Every decision here was a three-way tension between faculty clients, student users, and what the team could build.

01

Scoping three faculty ideas to one value prop

faculty enthusiasm vs. buildable scope

Three faculty concepts on the table, each defensible alone. Getting to one required identifying the strongest value proposition and saying no to the others. The hardest part was holding that line through faculty enthusiasm for their own concepts.

02

Merging Default Play and Performance Mode

faculty mental model vs. what users actually needed

An early version offered the two as separate menu options. Usability testing surfaced that the separation made the "all eyes on me" moment optional and opt-in, undermining the whole point. The redesign merged them into one continuous flow; the anxiety-inducing transition is now built in, not choosable.

03

Piano alignment: rejecting the first solution, A/B testing the second

schedule pressure vs. usability data

The first solution (manipulate a virtual cutout to match the keys) violated standard XR interaction patterns and consistently failed in usability testing. I called it, prototyped a second approach (a transparent cuboid overlay), and ran an A/B test. Results were mixed enough that neither won outright, and the team is now designing a hybrid. Sometimes the first workable solution isn't the right one to ship.

Synthesis from testing the early prototype keyboard setup screen. "8/8 participants" on the positioning note: that's the kind of signal that makes the call easy.

04

Setting up validation infrastructure before it was needed

institutional credibility vs. team capacity

Self-reported anxiety scores wouldn't be enough to validate whether the app actually works. I sought out a partnership with U-M's Institute for Social Research early — to design biometric validation into the eventual study before the team needed it. That partnership has IRB approval in place and has run pilot testing. The full impact study runs post-launch. Setting up institutional validation infrastructure before it was strictly needed is what makes the product defensible the day it ships.

Where it stands + what I learned.

As of April 2026, the project is on track for its June 2026 MVP. Both venues fully built, live audience, calibrated acoustics, Bluetooth MIDI routing, and the full pre-performance to performance flow are running. The load-bearing pieces are complete.

Holding the line on scope, with real clients

Scoping three competing faculty concepts down to one differentiated value proposition is a PM decision that looks like a writing exercise until you've tried it with real clients who have real enthusiasm for their own ideas. Holding that line while keeping the relationship intact: that's the job.

Let research lead

Faculty and students didn't always agree on what the product should prioritize, and research was the mechanism that resolved the difference. The testing on this project isn't a formality; it's the evidence trail for specific product decisions. Building that trail made it possible to align everyone around the same outcome.

Reading what the team can't do

A PM who identifies a capability gap before it becomes a credibility problem, and goes and finds the right people to fill it, is doing something different than one who waits. The ISR partnership happened because I read what the team couldn't do, not because someone handed me a spec.
